Frequently Asked Questions

Get immediate, direct answers to common questions about rural land acquisition, water basins, and assessor parcel searches in Arizona.

In Arizona, you must identify whether the land lies within an Active Management Area (AMA) where groundwater is strictly regulated, or in an Open Basin. You should always verify the existence of grandfathered water rights, surface water claims, or shared well agreements prior to completing a purchase.
Every available listing at Hague Creek includes a detailed LandID map integration. This allows buyers to interactively view property boundaries, soil topography, elevation profiles, parcel boundaries, and local utility access overlays directly on their screens.
An APN is the Assessor's Parcel Number, a unique identifier assigned to a specific parcel of land by the county assessor. It allows us to pull official tax records, verify legal descriptions, confirm zoning codes, and ensure the precise acreage matches the physical stakes.
Yes, raw land financing is available, though it typically requires higher down payments (often 20% to 50%) than residential home loans. We work closely with local agricultural credit associations and land lenders to secure favorable terms for qualified buyers.
Rural zoning in Arizona, such as RU-4 or RU-36, specifies the minimum acreage required for a single-family dwelling (e.g., 4 acres or 36 acres). Before purchasing, you should verify if the county zoning allows your intended use, such as livestock grazing, residential building, or off-grid homesteads.
You should check if the property has deeded legal access or is landlocked. Deeded access is recorded on the property title. If the parcel is accessed via private roads, you must confirm if there are recorded easement agreements allowing access, and check the physical road condition for vehicle suitability.
In Arizona, mineral rights are often severed from surface rights. Owning surface rights does not automatically grant you ownership of the subsurface minerals. A thorough title search is required to verify if the mineral rights are owned by a third party, the state, or the federal government.
Inside an AMA, groundwater withdrawal is strictly regulated by the Arizona Department of Water Resources (ADWR). If you plan to drill a well or subdivide land within an AMA, you must demonstrate a 100-year assured water supply, whereas areas outside AMAs (open basins) have fewer regulatory restrictions.
Raw land in Arizona is assessed based on its classification. Lands classified as agricultural (for farming or active grazing) receive significant tax reductions compared to vacant or residential-classified parcels. You must file an Agricultural Status Application with the county assessor to qualify.
A professional boundary survey verifies the exact corners, acreage, and identifies any physical encroachments (such as fences or structures built across lines) or unrecorded easements. Relying solely on county GIS maps can result in boundary disputes, as GIS maps are not legal surveys.
