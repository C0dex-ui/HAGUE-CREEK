Frequently Asked Questions

Direct answers on water basins, APNs, zoning, financing, and due diligence for Arizona land.

In Arizona, you must identify whether the land lies within an Active Management Area (AMA) where groundwater is strictly regulated, or in an Open Basin. Always verify grandfathered water rights, surface water claims, or shared well agreements prior to purchase.
Every available listing at Hague Creek includes LandID map integration so buyers can view property boundaries, soil topography, elevation profiles, parcel lines, and utility access overlays interactively.
An APN is the Assessor’s Parcel Number — a unique county identifier for a parcel. It unlocks tax records, legal descriptions, zoning confirmation, and acreage verification against physical stakes.
Yes. Raw land financing typically requires higher down payments (often 20% to 50%) than residential loans. We work with agricultural credit associations and land lenders for qualified buyers.
Codes such as RU-4 or RU-36 specify minimum acreage for a single-family dwelling (e.g. 4 or 36 acres). Verify that county zoning allows your intended use — grazing, residential building, or off-grid homestead.
Confirm deeded legal access on title. If access is via private roads, verify recorded easement agreements and inspect physical road condition for vehicle suitability so you are not landlocked.
Mineral rights are often severed from surface rights. Owning the surface does not automatically include subsurface minerals. A title search verifies if minerals are held by a third party, the state, or the federal government.
Inside an AMA, groundwater withdrawal is regulated by ADWR. Drilling a well or subdividing typically requires demonstrating a 100-year assured water supply. Open basins outside AMAs have fewer restrictions.
Raw land is assessed by classification. Agricultural status for farming or active grazing can reduce taxes significantly versus vacant residential classification. File an Agricultural Status Application with the county assessor.
A professional boundary survey verifies corners, acreage, and encroachments. County GIS maps are not legal surveys and can lead to boundary disputes if relied on alone.
